# Stock Market Guide

## 1. Introduction to the Stock Market

A **stock market** is a marketplace where investors buy and sell shares of publicly listed companies. When you buy a share, you become a partial owner of that company.

### Purpose of Stock Market

- Helps companies raise capital.
- Allows investors to earn returns.
- Provides liquidity for buying and selling shares.
- Supports economic growth.

### Key Participants

- Retail Investors
- Institutional Investors
- Brokers
- Stock Exchanges
- Regulators
- Listed Companies

---

## 2. NSE and BSE

### National Stock Exchange (NSE)

The NSE is India's largest stock exchange by trading volume.

**Features**

- Established in 1992.
- Introduced electronic trading.
- Benchmark Index: Nifty 50.
- Highly liquid market.

### Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE)

The BSE is Asia's oldest stock exchange.

**Features**

- Established in 1875.
- Benchmark Index: Sensex.
- More listed companies than NSE.

### Difference Between NSE and BSE


| Feature        | NSE               | BSE        |
| -------------- | ----------------- | ---------- |
| Founded        | 1992              | 1875       |
| Index          | Nifty 50          | Sensex     |
| Liquidity      | Higher            | Lower      |
| Trading Volume | Very High         | Moderate   |
| Technology     | Modern Electronic | Electronic |

## 3. Initial Public Offering (IPO)

An IPO occurs when a private company offers shares to the public for the first time.

### Purpose

- Raise capital.
- Expand business.
- Reduce debt.
- Increase brand visibility.

### IPO Process

1. Company hires investment banks.
2. SEBI approval obtained.
3. Prospectus published.
4. Investors apply.
5. Shares allotted.
6. Company gets listed.

### Types of IPO

#### Fixed Price IPO

Price is predetermined.

#### Book Building IPO

Investors bid within a price range.

### Benefits

- Early investment opportunity.
- Potential listing gains.

### Risks

- Overvaluation.
- Market volatility.

## 4. Market Capitalization

Market Capitalization represents the total value of a company.

### Formula

Market Cap=Share Price×Total Outstanding Shares\text{Market Cap} = \text{Share Price} \times \text{Total Outstanding Shares}Market Cap=Share Price×Total Outstanding Shares

### Categories

#### Large Cap

- Above ₹20,000 Crore.
- Stable companies.
- Lower risk.

#### Mid Cap

- ₹5,000–20,000 Crore.
- Moderate growth.

#### Small Cap

- Below ₹5,000 Crore.
- High growth potential.
- Higher risk.

### Example

If share price = ₹100 and shares outstanding = 10 Crore

Market Cap = ₹1000 Crore

## 5. Bull Market

A Bull Market refers to a prolonged rise in stock prices.

### Characteristics

- Investor confidence is high.
- Economy is growing.
- Corporate profits increase.
- Positive market sentiment.

### Signs

- Increasing stock prices.
- High trading volumes.
- Strong GDP growth.

### Example

Indian market rally from 2020–2024.

---

## 6. Bear Market

A Bear Market refers to a prolonged decline in stock prices.

### Characteristics

- Pessimistic investors.
- Economic slowdown.
- Falling company profits.
- High unemployment.

### Signs

- Continuous price declines.
- Market falls more than 20%.
- Low investor confidence.

### Causes

- Recession.
- Financial crises.
- Global conflicts.
- Pandemics.

## 7. Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental Analysis determines a stock's intrinsic value using company financials.

### Purpose

To find undervalued and overvalued stocks.

### Factors Analyzed

#### Revenue

Total company sales.

#### Profit

Net earnings after expenses.

#### Debt

Amount owed by the company.

#### Cash Flow

Money entering and leaving business.

#### Management Quality

Leadership effectiveness.

#### Industry Growth

Future market opportunities.

### Financial Statements

#### Balance Sheet

Assets, liabilities, equity.

#### Income Statement

Revenue and profit.

#### Cash Flow Statement

Cash movements.

### Important Ratios

- P/E Ratio
- EPS
- ROE
- Debt-to-Equity Ratio
- Current Ratio

### Advantages

- Long-term investing.
- Identifies quality companies.

### Disadvantages

- Time-consuming.
- Financial reports may lag.

## 8. Technical Analysis

Technical Analysis predicts future price movement using historical data.

### Based On

- Price
- Volume
- Market Trends

### Assumptions

1. Market discounts everything.
2. Price moves in trends.
3. History repeats itself.

### Common Tools

#### Support

Price level where buying increases.

#### Resistance

Price level where selling increases.

#### Trend Lines

Show market direction.

#### Moving Averages

Identify trends.

#### RSI

Measures momentum.

#### MACD

Trend-following indicator.

#### Bollinger Bands

Measure volatility.

### Advantages

- Useful for short-term trading.
- Provides entry and exit points.

### Disadvantages

- Not always accurate.
- Can generate false signals.

## 10. Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio

P/E Ratio measures how much investors are willing to pay for ₹1 of earnings.

### Formula

P/E=Market Price per ShareEarnings Per ShareP/E = \frac{\text{Market Price per Share}}{\text{Earnings Per Share}}P/E=Earnings Per ShareMarket Price per Share​

### Interpretation

#### High P/E

- High growth expectations.
- May be overvalued.

#### Low P/E

- Undervalued stock.
- Or poor future prospects.

### Example

Share Price = ₹500

EPS = ₹25

P/E = 20

Meaning investors pay ₹20 for every ₹1 of earnings.

---

## 11. Earnings Per Share (EPS)

EPS measures company profitability per share.

### Formula

EPS=Net Profit−Preferred DividendsOutstanding SharesEPS = \frac{\text{Net Profit} - \text{Preferred Dividends}}{\text{Outstanding Shares}}EPS=Outstanding SharesNet Profit−Preferred Dividends​

### Example

Net Profit = ₹100 Crore

Outstanding Shares = 10 Crore

EPS = ₹10

### Importance

- Indicates profitability.
- Used in stock valuation.
- Essential for P/E calculation.

### Types

#### Basic EPS

Uses current shares.

#### Diluted EPS

Includes potential future shares.
